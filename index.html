Current Research

Effect of monitoring system design on response time to cardiac arrhythmias

In US hospitals each year, approximately 370,000 to 750,000 emergency procedures that combine chest compressions and artificial ventilation to maintain blood circulation for a person in cardiac arrest (cardiopulmonary resuscitations). Unfortunately, around 80% of these patients do not survive to discharge, and 30% of patients where defibrillations are delayed more than two minutes reduces their chance of survival in half. The American Heart Association recommends defibrillation therapy within two minutes of cardiac arrest onset. For years, there are few reliable pieces of evidence to provide guidance on how to promptly respond to potentially fatal arrhythmia for an at-risk patient. Our objective is to identify and test the determinants of effective cardiac monitoring configurations. Simulation of cardiac arrhythmias in real hospital units allows us to measure the effect of these factors on response times without putting patients at risk. In this contribution, we will first compare 4 different hospitals, a total of 10 monitoring configurations, including remote telemetry, local monitoring by nurses, and local monitoring with automated notifications across multiple hospitals. The results will show the key factors that affect response time and will inform efforts to develop and study interventions to improve response time to cardiac arrhythmias and will ultimately help to develop evidence-based monitoring standards.
